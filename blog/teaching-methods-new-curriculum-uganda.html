Effective Teaching Methods for the New Curriculum in Uganda

In Uganda, the introduction of the new Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC) by the National Curriculum Development Centre (NCDC) has ushered in a fundamental shift in teaching methodologies. Replacing the traditional, content-heavy approach focused on rote memorization, this new lower secondary curriculum prioritizes skills, critical thinking, and real-world application of knowledge. For teachers, this means moving from being the sole source of information to becoming facilitators of a more dynamic, learner-centered process.

Key teaching methods in the new curriculum

Learner-centered methods

This is the central pillar of the new curriculum. Instead of teachers dictating information, students are actively engaged in their learning. Methods include:

  • Active participation: Students are encouraged to interact, discuss, and express their ideas without fear, fostering confidence and deeper understanding.

  • Inquiry and exploration: Teachers use a "stimulus" at the beginning of a lesson, such as a question or an experiment, to get students thinking and exploring concepts themselves.

Project-based learning (PBL)

This methodology requires students to investigate and respond to real-world problems through hands-on, long-term projects.

  • Real-world application: Students engage in projects that are relevant to their local environment, like addressing a community problem or developing an entrepreneurial idea.

  • Continuous assessment: The projects contribute a significant portion (around 10% in some models) to the students' final grades, ensuring consistent effort and skill development.

Integrative learning

The new curriculum promotes interdisciplinary learning, encouraging connections between subjects to create a more comprehensive understanding. For example, a history lesson might be linked with ICT, or science topics might be connected with environmental studies. This moves students away from learning in isolated silos and helps them see how knowledge applies across different contexts.

ICT integration

Digital literacy is a core component of the new curriculum. Teachers are expected to facilitate learning using digital tools and resources. This is critical for preparing students for a digitized world and ensuring they develop the necessary skills for a modern workforce. For example, lessons may involve multimedia presentations, online research, or basic coding exercises.

The crucial role of assessment management

With the shift to a competency-based model, the assessment process has also changed dramatically. Assessment is no longer just about a final exam; it now includes continuous assessment throughout the term, which is weighted into the final national exam grade.

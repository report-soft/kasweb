Understanding UNEB's "Elements of Construct" in Uganda's New Curriculum

The Uganda National Examinations Board (UNEB) has introduced a new approach to assessment under the new Lower Secondary Curriculum. A key feature of this system is the use of "elements of construct," a concept central to how students are taught and later examined for their Uganda Certificate of Education (UCE). Understanding these constructs is important for students as it guides them in their preparation for UCE examinations.

What Are the "Elements of Construct"?

An element of construct is a grouping of related topics or areas of content within a specific subject. Instead of testing isolated facts, UNEB now uses these broader themes to structure examination papers. This approach moves beyond rote memorization, focusing instead on a student's ability to apply knowledge and demonstrate a deep understanding of core subject competencies.

Uganda's New Curriculum aims to foster critical thinking and practical skills. By organizing the syllabus into these thematic constructs, UNEB ensures that assessment is comprehensive and aligned with modern pedagogical principles. Each construct outlines specific learning outcomes and competencies that a learner is expected to master.

How Elements of Construct Shape UCE Exams

The structure of the O-Level examinations is now directly linked to these elements. UNEB item banks are populated with questions designed to test a student's grasp of each specific construct. For example, a Biology exam paper is split into different sections based on constructs like "Diversity of living things" or "Human physiology."

This systematic approach provides clarity for both examiners and candidates. The marking schemes are precisely developed around the criteria defined by each construct, ensuring fair and consistent evaluation of student performance. It allows teachers to focus their instruction on these key thematic areas, moving away from a topic-by-topic, surface-level teaching method.
